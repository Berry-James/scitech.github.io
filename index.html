Light and Lasers

What is a Laser?

Laser is an acronym for light amplification by stimulated emission of radiation.

A laser beam is created when electrons within atoms in special gases absorb energy from an electrical current. When these electrons return to their normal state, they emit photons which create light. These photons are all the same wavelength, making them coherent or in other words the same colour.

What Makes a Laser?

Lasers are made up of many small components which work together to produce light.

A high-voltage electric supply makes a lamp flash on and off. Every flash releases photons into the ruby crystal. Atoms inside the ruby crystal absorb the energy through their electrons, which rapidly absorb and release the photons, creating light. These photons fly around the ruby crystal at the speed of light and stimulate already energised atoms, producing a second photon of light. The mirror at the end of the tube keeps the photons bouncing around inside the crystal, whilst the partial mirror bounces only some photons back and lets other escape. The escaping photons form a powerful laser beam.

What Can We Do With Lasers?

Lasers have many uses in our modern world, including:

  • Spectroscopy
  • Astronomy
  • Barcode scanning
  • Nuclear fusion
  • Surgery
  • Computer mice
  • CD readers
  • Laser tracking and cutting
